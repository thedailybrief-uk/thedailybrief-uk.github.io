Geopolitical

Israel Expands Ground Offensive in Lebanon as Abu Dhabi Casualty Confirmed

↻ This morning: Iran missile barrages on central Israel → This evening: Israel deploys two additional divisions into southern Lebanon; one killed in Abu Dhabi strike

Israel announced it is “expanding” its ground offensive in southern Lebanon, deploying two additional divisions to join the three already operating across the border. Troops from the 91st Division entered five towns including Kfar Kila, Yaroun and Khiam. More than 800,000 people have been displaced from southern Lebanon, with at least 850 killed including 107 children. In the Gulf, one person was confirmed killed by an Iranian missile in Al Bahyah, Abu Dhabi, taking the UAE’s total death toll to seven with 142 injuries.

Dive deeper
The expansion of the Lebanon ground operation represents a significant escalation from the “limited and targeted” operations announced earlier this week. Five divisions committed to southern Lebanon is more than Israel deployed during the 2006 war. The objective appears to be clearing all territory south of the Litani River, but this risks a protracted occupation against Hezbollah forces intimately familiar with the terrain. The Abu Dhabi casualty — a Palestinian national — underscores how the Gulf states have been drawn into a war they did not seek. Dubai International Airport resumed operations after an Iranian drone struck a fuel tank near the runway.

Trump Demands Allied Warships for Hormuz — Nobody Answers

↻ This morning: Trump warns NATO of ‘very bad future’ → This evening: names Starmer directly; UK offers drones instead of warships

President Trump called on seven nations to send warships to help reopen the Strait of Hormuz, through which 13 million barrels of oil passed daily before the conflict. None have publicly committed. Japan’s PM Takaichi told parliament Tokyo is “not planning to deploy”; Germany’s Defence Minister Pistorius insisted “this is not our war.” Starmer responded that the UK “will not be drawn into the wider war” but is “working with allies” on a plan. Energy Secretary Miliband said London is exploring deploying unmanned mine-sweeping systems.

Dive deeper
Trump’s warning — “We will remember” — carries real weight: the US controls the security umbrella under which most allied navies operate. Yet the demand exposes a fundamental contradiction: Washington launched the war that provoked Iran’s closure of the strait, then demands allies bear the burden of reopening it. Britain’s offer of unmanned systems rather than warships is a diplomatic compromise, but Trump is unlikely to regard it as sufficient. The Royal Navy withdrew its last Gulf frigate in 2025, leaving Britain unable to contribute meaningfully to any manned escort mission.

Brent Crude Eases Below $103 as Bessent Signals Partial Strait Access

↻ This morning: oil surges past $106 → This evening: drops to $102 after Treasury Secretary signals some tankers being permitted through

Brent crude fell roughly $4 to $102 per barrel after US Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent indicated some Iranian tankers were being permitted through the Strait of Hormuz. Tanker traffic remains down 70 per cent, with over 150 vessels anchored outside the strait. The ADNOC Ruwais refinery in Abu Dhabi — capacity 922,000 barrels per day — remains shut after a drone strike caused a major fire. Iran claims it has fired approximately 700 missiles and 3,600 drones since hostilities began.

Dive deeper
The oil price retreat is fragile and contingent on Bessent’s assurances holding. Iran’s selective transit policy — permitting Turkish, Indian and Saudi vessels whilst blocking Western-flagged shipping — is strategically shrewd, preventing a united international response. The Ruwais refinery shutdown removes nearly one million barrels per day of refining capacity from the global market. Analysts warn that if the strait remains closed beyond April, Brent could reach $130 and UK household energy bills may approach £2,500, levels not seen since the 2022–23 emergency.

Ukraine Leverages Drone Expertise to Help Gulf Allies Counter Iran

Ukraine is leveraging its battle-tested drone expertise to help the US and Gulf allies counter Iranian UAVs, positioning itself as an indispensable Western partner even as its own war grinds on. Russia has assisted Tehran with drone tactics informed by its own use of Iranian-designed Shahed UAVs against Ukrainian cities. On the battlefield, February became the first month since 2024 when Ukraine regained more territory than it lost, with Russian forces losing 57 square miles over four weeks.

Dive deeper
The symbiotic relationship is striking: Iran supplies Russia with Shahed drones, Russia shares tactics with Tehran, and Ukraine — having learned to counter those same drones — now advises the Gulf states facing them. The EU unanimously renewed sanctions against 2,600 Russian individuals and entities until September, the first unanimous vote since 2024, suggesting the Iran conflict has paradoxically strengthened European solidarity on Russia. Moscow benefits from surging energy prices but faces a newly resilient Ukrainian front line.

Trump Pivots to Section 301 Tariffs After Supreme Court Blocks IEEPA Levies

After the US Supreme Court ruled on 20 February that the President cannot use IEEPA to impose tariffs, the Trump administration launched Section 301 trade investigations targeting the EU, China, Mexico, Japan, South Korea, India and Taiwan. A blanket 10 per cent tariff remains on all trading partners. The Tax Foundation estimates the tariff regime amounts to the largest US tax increase as a percentage of GDP since 1993, costing the average American household $1,500 in 2026.

Dive deeper
The legal pivot from IEEPA to Section 301 buys the administration time but creates uncertainty for businesses. Section 301 investigations typically take 6–12 months, during which existing tariffs remain in force. Combined with the oil shock from the Iran conflict, the trade barriers are compounding inflationary pressures worldwide. The EU has signalled it will retaliate with targeted tariffs on American agricultural exports if Section 301 duties are imposed. For the UK, the 10 per cent blanket tariff adds roughly £4 billion to the annual cost of exporting to the US.

UK Domestic Politics

Starmer Announces £53m Heating Oil Support as Costs Double

At a Downing Street press conference, PM Keir Starmer announced £53 million in emergency support for 1.5 million vulnerable households reliant on heating oil, which is not covered by the energy price cap. In Northern Ireland, where 62 per cent of homes use oil heating, the cost of 900 litres surged from £537 on 26 February to £1,037 by 12 March. The Government has also capped energy bills until June (saving average households £117), extended the fuel duty cut to September, and released emergency oil stocks at “unprecedented” levels.

Dive deeper
The package addresses a gap in the existing safety net: the Ofgem price cap covers gas and electricity but not heating oil, which 1.5 million homes — predominantly in rural areas — rely upon. Northern Ireland is disproportionately affected, with costs nearly doubling in a fortnight. Starmer warned that suppliers caught cancelling orders or inflating prices will face legal action following a CMA investigation. The Opposition criticised the measures as “too little, too late,” arguing the Government should have anticipated the oil shock when it voted to support American strikes on Iran.

Bank of England Decision Looms Thursday: Markets Split on Rate Cut

↻ This morning: markets priced 70% hold → This evening: now evenly split as oil eases; traders swinging between cuts and hikes

The Monetary Policy Committee meets Thursday with markets evenly split on whether rates will be cut from 3.75 to 3.5 per cent. The February vote was 5–4 to hold, with minutes describing the decision as “finely balanced.” However, the oil shock has complicated the picture: UK CPI stands at 3.0 per cent, and Bloomberg noted traders have swung from pricing in cuts to betting on rate hikes as oil passed $100. The OBR cut 2026 GDP growth from 1.4 to 1.1 per cent.

Dive deeper
The MPC faces an acute dilemma: deteriorating growth data argues for easing, but surging energy costs risk reigniting inflation. GDP rose just 0.1 per cent in Q4 2025. Mortgage approvals fell 10 per cent year-on-year in January to 59,999, suggesting the housing market is cooling. The OBR’s Spring Forecast was finalised before the Iran conflict escalated and acknowledged the war “could have very significant impacts.” Tracker mortgage holders face several more months of elevated repayments regardless of Thursday’s outcome.

Government Launches £1bn Youth Jobs Scheme as Unemployment Hits 16.1%

Unemployment among 16–24 year olds has reached 16.1 per cent — nearly 957,000 young people — the highest rate in a decade and the first time UK youth unemployment has overtaken the EU average of 14.7 per cent. The Government announced a £1 billion Youth Jobs Grant aiming to create 200,000 positions. Businesses will receive £3,000 for each 18–24 year old hired who has been searching for work for six months or more. SMEs will be offered £2,000 per new apprentice.

Dive deeper
The scale of youth unemployment is alarming: nearly one million young people are out of work, with graduates particularly affected as employers facing rising wage costs favour experienced candidates. Job vacancies fell to 726,000 in the November 2025–January 2026 period, the lowest since 2021. The £1 billion package echoes Gordon Brown’s Future Jobs Fund but at greater scale. Critics argue the £3,000 hiring subsidy may simply displace older workers rather than create genuinely new roles. The scheme launches in September 2026.

Immigration Overhaul: Refugees Face 20-Year Path to Settlement

From 2 March 2026, successful asylum claimants now receive temporary protection status renewable every 30 months, replacing the previous five-year route to Indefinite Leave to Remain. Refugees become eligible for settlement only after 20 years, though a “work and study” route could reduce this to 10 years. The Home Affairs Select Committee has warned the retrospective application to those already resident is legally contentious. Most routes will require B2-level English from March 2027.

Dive deeper
The “earned settlement” framework represents the most significant tightening of immigration policy since Brexit. The 20-year default path effectively means many refugees will spend decades in a precarious legal status, unable to buy property or access certain benefits. The Home Affairs Committee published a critical report on 13 March warning of severe consequences for the adult social care workforce, which relies heavily on migrant labour. A Westminster Hall debate on the reforms is scheduled for Tuesday. Labour’s poll ratings suggest voters remain unconvinced despite the tightening.

Grenfell Memorial Bill Passes All Commons Stages

↻ This morning: Bill fast-tracked through Commons → This evening: all stages passed with cross-party support

The Grenfell Tower Memorial (Expenditure) Bill passed all Commons stages today in a single four-hour sitting, authorising public funds to build a permanent memorial to the 72 people who died in the 2017 fire. The site, covering just under 3,000 square metres including the tower footprint and adjacent land, will be transferred to a body determined by the Grenfell community. Parts of the tower structure will be preserved and archived.

Dive deeper
Nearly nine years after the fire, the legislation represents a belated fulfilment of promises made to bereaved families. Cross-party support was unanimous, reflecting the unique moral weight the Grenfell disaster carries in British politics. The Grenfell Tower Memorial Commission has been consulting survivors and the local community on design. The Bill provides a legal basis for managing the site permanently. Separately, the Iran conflict has exposed significant gaps in UK military capability — defence analysts warn Britain has no domestic ballistic missile defence system and could not intercept a ballistic missile aimed at London.

Davey Calls for Independent British Nuclear Deterrent

Developing a domestic replacement would cost tens of billions and take years. Co-operation with France — the only other European nuclear power — would reduce costs but create a different dependency. The call reflects a broader recalibration of UK security assumptions as Trump’s unpredictability forces allies to consider self-reliance.

Israel Launches ‘Broad Wave’ of Tehran Strikes; Explosion Rocks State Rally

↻ This morning: 200+ targets hit overnight → This evening: ‘broad wave’ strikes continue; explosion at Al Quds Day rally near senior officials

Israel’s military announced a fresh “broad wave” of strikes across Tehran on Friday after hitting more than 200 targets in western and central Iran over the previous day, including ballistic missile launchers and weapons-production sites. An explosion struck near a state-organised Al Quds Day rally attended by President Pezeshkian and security chief Larijani, killing at least one person. Israel has now carried out 7,600 strikes in Iran since 28 February.

Dive deeper
The targeting of central Tehran during a major public event represents a significant escalation in the psychological dimension of the campaign. Iran’s Health Ministry reports 1,444 people killed and 18,551 injured since the war began. The IDF claims 90 per cent of Iran’s air defences have been degraded, yet Tehran’s retaliatory capacity from hardened underground missile stores remains significant. Defence Secretary Hegseth said he believes new supreme leader Mojtaba Khamenei is “wounded” and possibly “disfigured”, though this remains unverified.

All Six KC-135 Crew Confirmed Dead; US Death Toll Reaches 13

↻ This morning: four crew reported killed → This evening: all six confirmed dead; US toll now 13

CENTCOM confirmed all six crew members aboard the KC-135 Stratotanker that crashed in western Iraq on Thursday have been killed, bringing the total American death toll to 13 since Operation Epic Fury began. The Islamic Resistance in Iraq claimed responsibility, though the Pentagon maintains the loss was not caused by hostile fire and may have involved a mid-air collision with a second tanker.

Dive deeper
The discrepancy between the militia’s claim and the Pentagon’s account remains unresolved. The second KC-135 made an emergency landing at Ben Gurion Airport in Israel. An investigation is underway but the loss underscores the operational strain of a campaign spanning multiple theatres. The KC-135 fleet is the backbone of US aerial refuelling; sustained attrition would constrain sortie rates. More than 250 US organisations have signed a letter calling on Congress to halt funding for the war, arguing the $11.3 billion spent in the first six days diverts funds from domestic needs.

Israel Destroys Litani Bridge; Warns Lebanon Faces Gaza-Scale Destruction

↻ This morning: Israeli strikes continue in Lebanon → This evening: Litani bridge destroyed; leaflets warn of Gaza-level devastation

Israel struck the Zrarieh Bridge spanning the Litani River in southern Lebanon and dropped leaflets over Beirut warning the country faces the same scale of destruction visited upon Gaza. Israeli attacks killed more than 25 people in Beirut and southern Lebanon on Friday, striking areas of the capital not hit in this conflict or the 2024 war. The Lebanese death toll stands at 773, with 800,000 displaced.

Dive deeper
The destruction of the Litani crossing is designed to sever Hezbollah’s supply lines between northern and southern Lebanon. Israel says it has carried out 1,100 strikes in Lebanon since 28 February. Lebanese President Joseph Aoun declared Hezbollah’s military activities outlawed and demanded the group confine itself to the political sphere — a remarkable shift for a country where Hezbollah has operated as a state within a state for decades. Hezbollah has vowed an “existential” fight, but its leadership has been severely degraded by targeted killings.

GCC Submits UN Resolution Demanding Iran Halt Gulf Attacks

All six Gulf Cooperation Council states submitted a draft UN Security Council resolution demanding Iran “immediately and unconditionally” cease all missile and drone attacks on neighbouring countries. Bahrain tabled the resolution on behalf of Saudi Arabia, Kuwait, Oman, Qatar and the UAE. Separately, Oman’s Sultan Haitham spoke with President Pezeshkian, calling for “the language of dialogue and diplomacy” to end the conflict.

Dive deeper
The unified GCC front at the Security Council is significant: these states rarely act in concert on military matters. Iran has struck civilian infrastructure across all six member states, damaging freshwater resources, airports, hotels and shopping centres. Qatar and Oman retain diplomatic channels with Tehran and are positioned as potential mediators. Russia and China, both permanent Security Council members, have yet to indicate whether they will veto the resolution. A 2,200-strong US Marine expeditionary unit is deploying from Okinawa to support operations and potential evacuation missions in the Gulf.

Putin Stalls on Ukraine Ceasefire; Russia Fires 559 Drones and Missiles

↻ This morning: 95% peace framework agreed → This evening: Putin rejects ceasefire; massive overnight strike on Kyiv

Vladimir Putin told US envoy Keith Kellogg there were “issues to resolve” before Russia could accept a ceasefire, effectively rejecting the Trump–Zelenskyy peace framework. Hours later, Russia launched 519 drones and 40 missiles at Ukraine, killing civilians and damaging energy infrastructure in Kyiv. Secretary Rubio said “the ball is now in Russia’s court”.

Dive deeper
The massive strike — one of the deadliest in months — targeted residential buildings and energy infrastructure, leaving parts of Kyiv without electricity or heating. More than ten residential buildings were damaged and rescue teams searched rubble for survivors. Russia’s foot-dragging is strategically rational: the Iran war boosts Moscow’s oil revenues while consuming American diplomatic bandwidth. The Urals blend has climbed above $70 per barrel, well above the $59 assumed in Russia’s 2026 budget. European leaders warned that prolonged US involvement in the Gulf serves Moscow’s strategic interests.

UK Domestic Politics

UK Economy Flatlines in January; Sterling Falls Below $1.33

↻ This morning: GDP data awaited → This evening: ONS confirms 0% growth; economy stalled since June

The Office for National Statistics confirmed UK GDP showed zero growth in January 2026, missing the 0.2 per cent consensus forecast. The dominant services sector was flat; production fell 0.1 per cent. British GDP has not grown since June, stalling at the same level for six consecutive months. Sterling fell 0.6 per cent to $1.3266 as ING warned the outlook is “becoming more challenging” with elevated energy prices.

Dive deeper
The data is particularly concerning because it predates the Iran conflict, which has since pushed Brent crude above $100 and introduced a major new headwind. Economists at Capital Economics now model UK growth of just 0.6 per cent for 2026 if oil stays elevated — well below the OBR’s already-downgraded 1.1 per cent forecast. Bank of England rate cut expectations have been scaled back further: markets now price just a 25 per cent chance of a cut at next Thursday’s meeting. The combination of stalling growth and rising energy costs raises the spectre of stagflation.

Hereditary Peers Bill Clears Final Hurdle — 92 Lords to Leave After 700 Years

The House of Lords (Hereditary Peers) Bill passed its final parliamentary stage this week, completing a constitutional reform first attempted a quarter of a century ago. Up to 92 hereditary peers who held their seats by virtue of birth will leave the chamber at the end of this parliamentary session in May. Royal assent from King Charles III is expected imminently.

Dive deeper
The bill removes the exemption inserted by the Blair government in 1999 that allowed 92 hereditary peers to remain while broader Lords reform was completed. That broader reform never came, leaving the hereditary principle embedded in Parliament for another 27 years. The departing peers include dukes, earls and viscounts whose families have sat in the Lords since the medieval period. The Electoral Reform Society called it “an important victory for democracy”, though critics note the chamber remains entirely unelected even without the hereditary element.

Government Adopts First Anti-Muslim Hostility Definition

The Government published a non-statutory definition of anti-Muslim hostility on Monday — the first formal attempt to define such prejudice. Home Office statistics show anti-Muslim hate crimes at a record 4,478 in the year to March 2025, representing nearly half of all religious hate crimes. The definition covers people perceived to be Muslim, including Sikhs and Hindus targeted because of their appearance.

Dive deeper
The definition was developed by an independent working group appointed in 2025. It explicitly protects free speech: “ridiculing or insulting a religion, including Islam, or portraying it in a manner some adherents might find disrespectful” remains protected expression. The Muslim Council of Britain said the definition “falls short” by not using the term Islamophobia. Humanists UK welcomed the approach as preferable to earlier proposals that risked conflating criticism of Islam with bigotry against Muslims. The timing is politically sensitive given rising community tensions since the Iran conflict began.

BBC World Service Faces Funding Crisis as March Deadline Looms

The Public Accounts Committee said it was “deeply troubled” by the BBC World Service’s funding uncertainty, with the current FCDO arrangement expiring at the end of March. The Service’s budget fell 21 per cent in real terms between 2021 and 2026, while Russia and China spend a combined £6–8 billion annually on global media operations.

Dive deeper
The World Service reaches 365 million people weekly and remains the most trusted international broadcaster, but MPs warned it risks losing that position as hostile states expand their output. Russia’s RT and China’s CGTN have invested heavily in African and Asian language services — precisely the markets where the BBC has been forced to cut back. A last-minute funding deal may be announced next week, but the PAC criticised the Government for leaving the Service unable to plan or invest. The crisis is particularly acute during the Iran conflict, when accurate, independent reporting from the region is at a premium.

Overseas Dentists Exam Places Expanded Tenfold to Tackle Access Crisis

The Government announced a near-tenfold expansion of places on the Licence in Dental Surgery exam, unlocking thousands of fully qualified overseas-trained dentists currently unable to practise in the UK. Limited exam capacity has been the main bottleneck; many qualified dentists have waited years for a place. The move aims to address a dental access crisis that has left millions unable to find an NHS dentist.

Dive deeper
An estimated 12 million adults in England have been unable to secure an NHS dental appointment when they needed one. The shortage is most acute in rural and coastal areas, where practices have closed or stopped taking NHS patients entirely. The Royal College of Surgeons, which administers the exam, will expand testing capacity from roughly 400 places per year to nearly 4,000. The first additional cohort is expected to qualify by autumn 2026. Dental charities welcomed the announcement but warned that exam expansion alone will not solve the crisis without reforms to the NHS dental contract, which many practitioners consider financially unviable.

Four US Crew Killed as KC-135 Tanker Crashes in Iraq

A US Air Force KC-135 Stratotanker crashed in western Iraq on Thursday while supporting Operation Epic Fury, killing four crew members. The Pentagon said the crash was not caused by hostile fire; a second KC-135 made an emergency landing at Ben Gurion Airport. It is the first loss of a US tanker aircraft in thirteen years.

Dive deeper
The loss brings the American death toll to eleven since the campaign began on 28 February, with eight further personnel seriously wounded. The Islamic Resistance in Iraq claimed responsibility, contradicting official US accounts. The KC-135 fleet is the backbone of American aerial refuelling operations and any sustained attrition would constrain sortie rates. The crash underscores the operational strain of a campaign now spanning multiple theatres across the Middle East.

Iran Warns Oil Could Hit $200 as Three More Ships Struck

IRGC naval forces attacked three further commercial vessels in the Persian Gulf overnight, bringing the total to six ships hit in 48 hours. Iran’s oil ministry warned Brent crude could reach $200 per barrel if the conflict escalates. Brent settled at $100.84, its second consecutive close above $100.

Dive deeper
The widening of attacks to vessels in Iraqi territorial waters threatens Baghdad’s 3.3 million barrels per day of southern exports. Insurance premiums for Gulf-bound tankers have risen fifteenfold since the war began. Goldman Sachs raised its Q4 Brent forecast and warned spot prices could exceed the 2008 peak of $147 if Hormuz flows remain depressed. The International Maritime Organisation will convene an extraordinary session next week to address threats to Gulf shipping.

Pezeshkian Sets Three Conditions for Peace as Day 14 Begins

Iranian President Masoud Pezeshkian outlined three preconditions to end the war: recognition of Tehran’s legitimate rights, payment of reparations, and firm international guarantees against future aggression. His demands echo those of new supreme leader Mojtaba Khamenei, who vowed to keep the Strait of Hormuz closed.

Dive deeper
The alignment between the civilian president and the new supreme leader suggests a unified Iranian negotiating position, though Western intelligence agencies remain uncertain whether Mojtaba commands genuine authority over the IRGC. The maximalist demands — particularly reparations — are unlikely to find any traction in Washington. CENTCOM reports that approximately 6,000 targets have been struck inside Iran, with 90 per cent of air defences degraded, yet Tehran’s retaliatory capacity from underground missile stores remains significant.

UNHCR: 3.2 Million Iranians Displaced; Civilian Toll Passes 1,348

The United Nations refugee agency said up to 3.2 million Iranians have been forced from their homes since 28 February. Iran’s UN representative said at least 1,348 civilians have been killed as the conflict enters Day 14. Lebanese health officials report 687 dead from concurrent Israeli operations.

Dive deeper
The displacement figure places the humanitarian toll on a scale comparable to the early months of the Syrian civil war. Aid agencies warn that Iran’s infrastructure for sheltering internally displaced people is wholly inadequate, with many sleeping in vehicles or open fields. Seven US soldiers have died and eight are seriously wounded, mainly from retaliatory attacks on American bases in neighbouring countries. The crisis is compounded by Lebanon, where 750,000 people have also fled their homes.

Ukraine Hits Russian Electronics Plant Building Missile Parts

Ukraine used Storm Shadow missiles to strike a Russian factory producing microchips and high-end electronics for missile guidance systems. Separately, Presidents Trump and Zelenskyy confirmed agreement on 90–95 per cent of a Ukraine peace framework, though security guarantees remain unresolved.

The strike on the electronics plant marks a shift from isolated attacks on oil infrastructure to systematic targeting of Russia’s military-industrial base. Classified Russian documents obtained by Ukrainian intelligence reveal total irreversible losses exceeding 1.3 million personnel since February 2022. The near-agreement on a peace framework represents the most concrete diplomatic advance since the Istanbul talks, though European leaders remain cautious about any settlement that rewards territorial conquest.

UK Domestic Politics

Reeves Faces Treasury Committee Grilling on Iran Oil Shock

Chancellor Rachel Reeves appears before the Treasury Committee today alongside the OBR and IFS to defend the Spring Forecast. The OBR cut 2026 growth to 1.1 per cent before the conflict; City economists now model 0.6–0.8 per cent if Brent stays above $100. Rising gilt yields threaten to eliminate the £9.9bn fiscal buffer.

Dive deeper
The Spring Forecast’s assumptions have been rendered obsolete within days by the Iran crisis. The Resolution Foundation estimates sustained energy prices could add £500 to the typical household bill and a full percentage point to inflation. Reeves faces a trilemma at the autumn budget: breach her fiscal rules, announce further spending cuts, or raise taxes — any of which would compound Labour’s difficulties ahead of the May local elections.

Courts and Tribunals Bill Provokes Cross-Party Fury on Jury Trials

MPs debated the Courts and Tribunals Bill for the first time this week, with the legislation to remove defendants’ right to elect jury trial for either-way offences drawing fierce opposition. Nearly 40 Labour backbenchers have written to Starmer refusing to support the measure. The Law Society and Bar Council condemned the reforms.

Dive deeper
The bill would allow judge-only trials for offences carrying up to three years’ imprisonment, affecting one in four fraud cases. The Government argues it is essential to reduce the Crown Court backlog of 73,000 cases. Critics counter that removing jury trials undermines a constitutional right dating to Magna Carta. The Labour rebellion represents the most serious internal challenge to Starmer’s legislative programme since the general election.

Crime and Policing Bill Enters Final Report Stage Debates

The Crime and Policing Bill continues its report stage in the House of Lords, with peers debating amendments on knife crime, anti-social behaviour and violence against women. The bill creates new offences for possessing knives with intent and increases maximum penalties for selling prohibited weapons.

Dive deeper
The legislation is one of the most significant criminal justice measures of this Parliament, touching on issues from shoplifting to child sexual exploitation. Peers have tabled amendments seeking to strengthen protections for retail workers. The bill is expected to proceed to third reading before Easter, though several contentious amendments on protest rights remain unresolved after the Government suffered five defeats on the separate NI Pensions Bill this week.

Inheritance Tax Relief Reforms Take Effect in Three Weeks

The Government confirmed changes to Agricultural Property Relief and Business Property Relief from 6 April. The allowance rises from £1m to £2.5m following December’s partial U-turn; assets above the threshold face a 20 per cent effective rate. Spouses may transfer unused allowances, reducing affected estates from 375 to 185.

Dive deeper
The reforms remain contentious despite the increased threshold. The National Farmers’ Union argues that land values in many regions mean family farms will still be caught by the charge. Around 85 per cent of estates claiming agricultural relief are forecast to pay no additional tax, but the remaining 15 per cent face bills that could force asset sales. The changes represent the most significant reform of agricultural inheritance tax since the relief was introduced in 1992.

Defence Spending Pressure Mounts as Iran Conflict Enters Third Week

Cross-party pressure on Starmer to accelerate defence spending has intensified as the Iran war enters Day 14. Military chiefs warn of a £28bn budget shortfall; NATO allies agreed a 5 per cent of GDP target by 2035 at the February summit. The Chancellor has resisted, citing £9.9bn of headroom now under severe strain.

Dive deeper
The current commitment of 2.5 per cent of GDP by 2027 falls well short of what defence chiefs say is needed. Finding billions more without further tax rises appears arithmetically challenging after the Spring Statement already trimmed departmental budgets. The debate exposes a fundamental tension in Labour’s fiscal strategy between its domestic spending commitments and the rapidly deteriorating international security environment, with the Iran crisis consuming significant military resources.
