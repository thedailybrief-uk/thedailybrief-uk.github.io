Iran Says 10,000 Civilian Sites Struck as War Enters Day 12

Tehran claims US and Israeli forces have bombed nearly 10,000 civilian sites and killed more than 1,300 civilians since Operation Epic Fury began on 28 February. Explosions rocked Tehran, Tabriz, Karaj and Kish Island overnight. Defence Secretary Hegseth announced Tuesday would be “yet again, our most intense day of strikes”, deploying the largest sortie of fighters and bombers to date.

Dive deeper
Iran’s civilian casualty figures cannot be independently verified, but satellite imagery corroborates widespread infrastructure damage across multiple cities. The Pentagon insists its targeting is confined to military and IRGC assets; Tehran’s broader count includes dual-use infrastructure such as power stations, bridges and communications hubs. The intensification suggests Washington is pressing for maximum degradation of Iranian capabilities before political pressure forces a halt. Iran’s ballistic missile launches have fallen 90 per cent and drone launches 83 per cent, indicating the campaign is achieving its stated military objectives.

Gulf States Intercept Fresh Iranian Missile and Drone Waves

Saudi Arabia, Kuwait, Bahrain, Qatar and the UAE reported intercepting Iranian missiles and drones early on Wednesday local time. Bahrain confirmed it had destroyed 105 missiles and 176 drones since the war began; a 29-year-old woman was killed in Manama when a projectile struck a residential building. Saudi air defences intercepted four drones bound for the Shaybah oilfield. It is the first conflict in which Iran has struck all six GCC member states.

Dive deeper
The expansion of Iranian strikes to all GCC nations marks a dramatic escalation from Tehran’s initial posture, which pledged to spare neighbouring states unless they hosted offensive operations against Iran. The attacks appear designed to raise the cost of the Gulf states’ tacit support for US basing rights. Bahrain — home to the US Navy’s Fifth Fleet — has borne a disproportionate share of the bombardment. The Shaybah oilfield, deep in Saudi Arabia’s Empty Quarter, was previously targeted by Houthi drones in 2019; its vulnerability underscores the limits of even sophisticated air defence systems against saturation attacks.

Brent Stabilises Near $88 After Last Week’s $120 Spike

Brent crude traded at $87.95 on Wednesday morning, consolidating after last week’s violent swing from $120 to $87. A diplomatic delegation secured commitments to keep Hormuz shipping lanes partially open, and the US Navy announced escort missions for commercial tankers — though no vessel has yet completed a Navy-escorted transit. Iran continues to ship oil to China through the strait, with 11.7 million barrels dispatched since the war began.

Dive deeper
The $30 price collapse drained the “war premium” but analysts caution it may prove premature. Mine fears persist: ten vessels have been attacked in or near Hormuz, killing at least seven seafarers. Insurance underwriters have withdrawn war-risk cover for the strait, effectively doubling transit costs for those willing to attempt the passage. Iran’s continued oil exports to China — circumventing its own declared blockade — expose the selective nature of the strait closure and have drawn sharp criticism from Gulf producers unable to ship their own crude.

Brent’s surge above $100 last week pushed Russia’s Urals blend past $70 per barrel — well above the $59 assumed in Moscow’s 2026 budget. Dive deeper
The windfall from higher oil prices comes at a moment when Russia’s war economy was under strain. The Urals blend premium strengthens the Kremlin’s fiscal position and reduces incentives to accept a Ukraine ceasefire. Chatham House analysis notes that the conflict has paradoxically weakened Russia’s leverage in the Middle East — Moscow could not protect its Iranian partner, and its influence over regional outcomes has diminished. Lavrov has signalled that Russia will delay engagement with the Jeddah ceasefire framework, calculating that prolonged US involvement in the Gulf serves Moscow’s strategic interests.

UK Domestic Politics

Courts Bill Survives First Vote Despite Labour Rebellion

The Courts and Tribunals Bill cleared its second reading by 304 votes to 203, a majority of 101, despite ten Labour MPs voting against and 90 abstaining. The bill would replace juries with single judges for cases where the likely sentence is three years or fewer. Lord Chancellor Lammy argued the Crown Court backlog of 73,000 cases — including 2,000 rape trials waiting over a year — demanded radical reform.

Dive deeper
The rebellion is the largest under Starmer’s premiership and reflects deep unease on the backbenches about curtailing a constitutional right dating to Magna Carta. Karl Turner MP led the opposition, calling the changes a “dereliction of duty” without additional Crown Court funding. More than 3,200 lawyers — including 300 King’s Counsel and 22 retired judges — marched on Parliament ahead of the vote. The bill faces a far more hostile reception in the Lords, where cross-bench peers are expected to table significant amendments.

New Workers’ Rights Take Effect from 6 April

Statutory sick pay from day one, an end to exploitative zero-hours contracts, and day-one paternity and parental leave come into force on 6 April. The reforms extend SSP to 1.3 million low-paid workers previously excluded by the £125-per-week earnings threshold. The government also confirmed protections for pregnant workers and a new statutory right to bereavement leave.

Dive deeper
The Employment Rights Bill represents the most significant overhaul of workplace legislation in a generation. Employers’ groups have warned of increased costs, particularly for small businesses already contending with the National Insurance rise from April. The Federation of Small Businesses estimates the combined cost of new employment obligations at £4.5 billion annually. However, the TUC argues that reducing presenteeism — workers attending while ill — will boost productivity and reduce the spread of illness in workplaces.

Water Companies Sent Bailiffs to Thousands of Homes

The EFRA Committee published data showing water companies in England and Wales used bailiffs against indebted customers 35,000 times in 2024, up from 15,000 in 2020. Yorkshire Water recorded 6,124 bailiff visits in 2024–25 alone. Southern Water instructed bailiffs more than 8,000 times in 2024. The figures mark the first time this data has been made public, as water firms are exempt from the Freedom of Information Act.

Dive deeper
The scale of bailiff use against customers of an essential public service has prompted calls for regulatory reform. Water debt differs from other consumer debts because customers cannot switch provider — they are tied to a regional monopoly. Consumer groups argue that the post-pandemic surge in enforcement reflects a failure by companies to offer adequate hardship schemes. The revelation is politically significant as it coincides with public anger over sewage discharges and executive bonuses across the water industry.

Inheritance Tax Farm Relief Changes Take Effect Next Month

From 6 April, agricultural and business property attracting inheritance tax relief will be capped at £2.5 million per person at the full 100 per cent rate; assets above this threshold face an effective 20 per cent tax. Couples may pass on up to £5 million of combined farm and business assets. The Treasury estimates 185 estates will pay more IHT in 2026–27, while 85 per cent of claims will be unaffected.

Dive deeper
The changes, announced at the Autumn 2024 Budget, provoked months of protest from farming groups who argue that land values have inflated beyond the cap. The NFU contends that the £2.5 million threshold will force family farms to sell land to meet tax bills, accelerating consolidation. The government has responded with a concession allowing payments over ten annual interest-free instalments. Rural constituencies are politically sensitive for Labour ahead of the May local elections, and several Labour MPs in agricultural seats have voiced concern privately.

Rail Fare Freeze Saves Commuters up to £300 Per Year

The first rail fare freeze in three decades took effect across England, covering all regulated fares including season tickets and off-peak returns. The government estimates the freeze will save passengers £600 million in 2026–27, with commuters on the busiest routes saving more than £300 annually. The measure accompanies the ongoing renationalisation of rail services under Great British Railways.

Dive deeper
The freeze applies to approximately 45 per cent of fares — those regulated by the government — while unregulated fares set by train operators may still rise. The cost is borne by the Exchequer rather than operators, adding to the transport budget at a time of fiscal constraint. Transport economists note the freeze addresses a longstanding grievance: UK rail fares have risen 40 per cent in real terms since privatisation in 1993. The renationalisation programme has so far brought four operators under public control, with the remaining franchises due to transfer by 2028.
