Brent crude settled at $107.40, down 5.5 per cent on profit-taking.

Dive deeper
Iran’s strategy of targeting Gulf energy infrastructure is devastatingly effective asymmetric warfare. By striking allies’ energy assets rather than US military targets directly, Tehran is weaponising the economic consequences of the war. The Qatar LNG damage is particularly significant: a 17 per cent capacity reduction lasting five years would reshape global energy markets, with Europe — still weaning itself off Russian gas — facing acute supply competition with Asian buyers. Kuwait and the UAE, which have sought neutrality, now face domestic pressure to distance themselves from the US-led effort. This is Iran’s leverage: it cannot match US–Israeli military power, but it can make the war economically unbearable for everyone in the region. Watch for whether Gulf states pressure Washington for a ceasefire.

UK Domestic Politics

Petrol Prices Surge Past 144p as Oil Crisis Squeezes Households

UK petrol prices hit 144.8p per litre — the highest in over 18 months — with diesel at 166.1p, as the Iran conflict sends Brent crude above $107. Prices are up 10p per litre since 28 February. The 1.5 million households reliant on heating oil have been worst hit, with costs jumping over 80 per cent. Economists warn sustained $100+ oil could cut UK GDP growth by 0.5 percentage points.

Dive deeper
Britain is sleepwalking into a second energy crisis in four years. The April price cap reduction of £117 — announced to much fanfare — will be entirely wiped out if Cornwall Insight’s projection of a £160 July cap increase materialises. The 1.5 million homes dependent on heating oil face the sharpest pain — these are disproportionately rural properties in Scotland, Wales and Northern Ireland, where there is no mains gas alternative. Unlike the 2022 crisis, the Government has limited fiscal headroom for intervention; Rachel Reeves’ Spring Statement left just £9.9 billion of headroom against fiscal rules. If Brent stays above $100 through summer, Reeves may face an emergency fiscal statement. Watch for whether the Treasury announces targeted support for heating oil households.

Starmer’s position is increasingly untenable. Labour’s base is overwhelmingly opposed to Middle Eastern military engagement, and Reform UK — polling at 28 per cent — will exploit any escalation. If Britain is drawn deeper into a Hormuz escort mission, the costs escalate rapidly. Watch for whether Parliament gets a vote.

Dive deeper
This is a structural brain drain, not a cyclical blip. The 6,000 directors who have already left represent the leading edge of a capital flight that strips Britain of exactly the high-growth founders the Government claims to want. Dubai’s zero income tax, the US’s Inflation Reduction Act subsidies, and even France’s tech visa programme are actively courting disaffected British entrepreneurs. The 3 per cent confidence figure in government understanding is devastating — it suggests policy alienation so deep that incremental adjustments will not suffice. With the OBR projecting just 1.1 per cent growth, losing the sector most likely to generate above-trend productivity gains is an own goal of strategic proportions. Watch for whether the Government announces targeted enterprise measures before the Autumn Budget.

Reform Dominates Polls at 28% as Labour Hits Record Lows

Dive deeper
British politics is undergoing a realignment not seen since the 1920s collapse of the Liberal Party. The Greens’ surge to 21 per cent — validated by their historic Gorton and Denton by-election win — suggests the left’s disaffected voters are actively reorganising. The 8-in-10 figure believing Britain is “getting worse” reflects a systemic legitimacy crisis. Starmer’s minus 66 net approval is worse than any recorded for Thatcher, Major, Brown or Truss at their respective nadirs. Watch for whether Labour attempts a major policy reset before the next general election.

Unilever in Talks to Sell $33bn Food Arm to McCormick

Dive deeper
This deal carries significant implications for British industry. An all-stock transaction would shift the centre of gravity for these British heritage brands to Maryland. For the UK, the question is whether food division jobs — manufacturing, R&D, marketing — remain in Britain under American ownership. The deal also creates potential competition concerns: a combined Hellmann’s–French’s entity would dominate the global condiments market. Watch for CMA scrutiny and whether the UK Government intervenes on grounds of national interest.
