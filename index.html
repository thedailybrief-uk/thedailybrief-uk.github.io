Iran Strikes Qatar, Saudi and UAE Energy Sites After South Pars Hit

↻ This morning: Israel struck South Pars → This evening: Iran retaliates across three Gulf states

Iran launched missiles at energy infrastructure across Qatar, Saudi Arabia and the UAE in retaliation for Israel’s overnight strike on the South Pars gasfield. Qatar’s Ras Laffan LNG hub suffered “extensive damage,” knocking out 17 per cent of Qatari export capacity. Two Saudi refineries in Riyadh were struck. The IRGC warned further attacks would continue “until complete destruction.”

Dive deeper
The strikes represent a fundamental escalation — for the first time, Iran has attacked the energy infrastructure of three separate Gulf states simultaneously, transforming the bilateral US–Israel–Iran conflict into a regional energy war. Qatar’s Ras Laffan supplies roughly 25 per cent of global LNG exports; Saudi Arabia’s refineries process crude for both domestic consumption and export. The IRGC’s explicit promise of continued strikes until “complete destruction” signals Tehran is prepared to absorb the diplomatic cost of targeting non-belligerents. European gas prices surged 15 per cent as traders priced in prolonged disruption to LNG supply chains.

Dive deeper
Prince Faisal’s statement marks the sharpest break in Saudi–Iranian relations since the 2016 diplomatic severance — and arguably more significant, given the 2023 China-brokered rapprochement that was supposed to have buried the hatchet. The explicit reservation of military action goes further than any Gulf state has gone since the conflict began on 28 February. If Saudi Arabia or the UAE strike Iranian targets, the war expands from an Israeli–American campaign to a genuine regional conflict involving the world’s largest oil producers. The diplomatic question is whether Washington wants Gulf states fighting or restraining — Trump’s simultaneous threat to destroy South Pars suggests the former.

Dive deeper

The FTSE 100’s decline was concentrated in airlines, housebuilders and retailers — sectors most exposed to consumer spending pressures from higher energy costs. Defence stocks bucked the trend, with BAE Systems and Rolls-Royce both higher.

Dive deeper

The growth downgrade to 0.9 per cent confirms the UK is on the edge of stagnation, yet the MPC judged inflation risk too great to ease. For 1.4 million homeowners whose fixed deals expire this year, the practical consequence is that rate relief has been pushed to autumn at the earliest — and a hike cannot be ruled out if Brent stays above $110.

Dive deeper
The use of multiple-warhead cluster missiles marks a tactical escalation designed to overwhelm Israel’s Iron Dome system, which was engineered to intercept single-warhead projectiles. Each cluster munition forces multiple intercepts per incoming round, burning through interceptor stocks at three to four times the normal rate — a strategy Iran likely adopted after studying Hezbollah’s saturation tactics during the 2024 border exchanges. The Ramat Gan impact — less than three kilometres from central Tel Aviv — is the closest strike to Israel’s commercial heartland since the conflict began on 28 February, and the deadliest single hit on a residential building since the 2023–24 Gaza war. Iran’s choice of civilian population centres rather than military targets signals a deliberate shift towards psychological warfare aimed at eroding public morale and forcing internal political pressure on Netanyahu’s war cabinet. Rail disruption across the Dan metropolitan area — home to 3.8 million people — compounds the economic toll; the Tel Aviv Stock Exchange has lost 19 per cent since 28 February. Watch for whether Iron Dome interception rates, currently around 85 per cent, fall below the 70 per cent threshold at which civilian casualties would escalate sharply.

Larijani was the de facto operational leader after the elder Khamenei’s death on 28 February, controlling both foreign policy and the nuclear portfolio. Gholamreza Soleimani’s Basij force numbers an estimated 600,000 personnel and serves as the regime’s primary instrument for suppressing internal dissent — its loss at a time of sustained aerial bombardment raises the spectre of domestic unrest. The absence of any verified communication from Mojtaba Khamenei since his appointment on 8 March raises the prospect that effective authority has devolved to IRGC field commanders operating without central direction. Historically, revolutionary states that lose centralised command structures — Libya in 2011, Iraq in 2003 — fragment rapidly. The key indicator to watch is whether Iran’s retaliatory strikes become less coordinated, suggesting operational breakdown, or more reckless, suggesting commanders acting independently.

The AI Centre of Excellence could prove equally significant — Ukraine’s battlefield data on autonomous targeting is unmatched. Dive deeper
Saudi Arabia’s Yanbu workaround underscores the desperation of major producers to maintain supply, but the port can handle only about 2 million barrels per day — a quarter of the roughly 8 million bpd that previously transited Hormuz. The East–West pipeline connecting the Gulf coast to Yanbu was built after Iraq’s invasion of Kuwait in 1990 precisely for this scenario, yet it was never designed to replace the strait entirely. The IEA’s record 400-million-barrel reserve release is being drawn down at roughly 4 million barrels per day; at this rate, strategic reserves could fall below the 90-day minimum mandated by IEA membership rules by late summer, forcing rationing discussions. Asian economies remain most exposed — Vietnam and Pakistan hold less than 20 days of strategic supply, and India’s 63-day buffer is shrinking. For context, the 1973 Arab oil embargo removed only 4 million bpd from global supply and triggered a global recession; the current disruption is twice that scale. The trigger to watch is whether Saudi Arabia begins diverting crude from domestic refining to export via Yanbu, which would signal genuine panic about global supply adequacy.
