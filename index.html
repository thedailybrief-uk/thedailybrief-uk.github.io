Geopolitical

Trump’s 48-Hour Ultimatum Ticks Down; Iran Threatens Permanent Hormuz Closure

↻ This morning: ultimatum issued, 22-nation coalition forming → This evening: Iran counter-threatens permanent closure and regional infrastructure strikes

President Trump’s ultimatum demanding Iran reopen the Strait of Hormuz expires approximately 11:44 PM GMT on Monday. Iran’s military command responded today by declaring the Strait would be “completely closed indefinitely” if any power plants are struck, and that all US and Israeli energy, IT and desalination infrastructure across the region would be targeted. A 22-nation coalition — up from 7 on 19 March — has now signed a joint statement backing freedom of navigation, with the UAE confirming its participation today. Saudi Arabia expelled Iran’s military attaché and four embassy staff with a 24-hour departure deadline, citing attacks that “contradict Islamic brotherhood.”

Dive deeper
The next 30 hours are the most dangerous of the conflict. Trump has publicly committed to striking civilian energy infrastructure — a move that could constitute a war crime under Protocol I of the Geneva Conventions — while Iran has promised permanent Hormuz closure in response. Neither side has offered a diplomatic off-ramp. The 22-nation coalition represents significant non-military alignment but stops conspicuously short of committing to military action, leaving the US and Israel as the only combatants. Saudi Arabia’s expulsion of Iranian diplomats is a watershed: Riyadh had spent three years rebuilding ties with Tehran through Chinese-brokered diplomacy, and the expulsion signals that relationship is now dead. Iran’s selective Hormuz policy — allowing Japanese ships while blocking Western ones — is designed to fracture the coalition by creating economic incentives to break ranks. Watch for whether Oman or China emerge as mediators before the Monday night deadline, and whether the Pentagon positions assets for power plant strikes or whether the ultimatum quietly expires.

IAEA Confirms Natanz Bombing; Iran Death Toll Reaches 1,500

↻ This morning: strike confirmed, IAEA investigating → This evening: Grossi formally calls for “maximum military restraint near nuclear facilities”

IAEA Director General Rafael Grossi confirmed the Natanz enrichment facility was struck by what Iran attributes to US bunker-buster bombs, but found “no increase in off-site radiation levels.” Grossi called for “maximum military restraint, particularly in the vicinity of nuclear facilities” — a direct rebuke to both sides after Iran retaliated by striking near Israel’s Dimona nuclear research centre, wounding nearly 200. Russia condemned the Natanz strike as “a brazen violation of international law.” Iran’s total death toll has reached 1,500, including 204 children, with 20,984 injured and seven hospitals evacuated.

Dive deeper
Grossi’s intervention marks a pivotal moment: the world’s nuclear watchdog is publicly warning that both sides are targeting nuclear infrastructure in a tit-for-tat pattern that risks catastrophe. Iran had enriched uranium to 60 per cent purity — far beyond the 3–5 per cent needed for civilian power — giving the Natanz strike a nonproliferation justification the US will emphasise. But Iran’s retaliatory strikes near Dimona, where Israel is widely believed to maintain an undeclared nuclear arsenal, demonstrate the logic of escalation: if you hit our nuclear sites, we hit yours. The death toll of 1,500 including 204 children is approaching the scale at which international pressure for a ceasefire becomes overwhelming. Watch for whether China and Russia push for a UN Security Council emergency session, and whether the IAEA issues a formal finding on the Natanz damage assessment.
